Programming Note: The War on Drugs Turns 40

Changes are coming to this blog. I have decided that education is simply too confining a subject for me, and that I need to expand the scope and subject matter of my writing into other areas that are at least as important as the fate of our nation’s schools.

The most crucial issue facing our country and our liberty now and in the years to come is the expansion of the police state and the ratcheting up of the War on Drugs. This is a war that impacts not only Americans, but the rest of the world as well. It is an expensive, violent, and ultimately futile crusade fought largely against our own citizens, impacting everything from our schools to our prisons to the vitality of our economy and the global economy.

Mexico alone, since president Felipe Calderon began his war on drugs in December, 2006, over 34,000 people have died in drug war violence alone.

So many nonviolent offenders crowd our prison system that the US Supreme Court has ordered California to release nearly a quarter of its prison population.

Meanwhile, across the United States paramilitary S.W.A.T. teams raid both violent and non-violent offender’s homes, often just to serve a warrant. Too often these raids result in tragedy.

It seems a fitting time to switch my beat – just as the War on Drugs turns 40.

Nothing in our national history has been more expensive, more damaging to individual liberty, and more wasteful of taxpayer dollars than the War on Drugs. It is waged both within our borders and without. It grows in intensity and cost every year, and it saps us of important resources that should be devoted to more important crime-fighting such as human smuggling, or to drug treatment. Decades after Nixon launched his war on the drug “menace” we are still waging it and it is still a lost cause. Under the current administration, it has only intensified.

Entire communities have been disrupted by the violence stemming from prohibition. This was the case during alcohol prohibition as well, when organized crime emerged in full force and more and more good money was thrown after bad. Eventually, Americans rightly revolted against that nonsense. It appears a shift is underway in our collective conscious once again.

Two candidates for the 2012 GOP nomination support an end to prohibition: Ron Paul and Gary Johnson. Members of both parties are beginning to see the destructiveness of our drug war policies.

It’s time to stop repeating the mistakes of history. Too much money, too many lives, and too many of our liberties are at stake. I hope to cover some of this ground here going forward, following in the footsteps of journalists like Radley Balko who has been a true trailblazer on this subject.

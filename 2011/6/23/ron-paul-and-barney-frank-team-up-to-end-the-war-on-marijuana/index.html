Ron Paul and Barney Frank Team Up to End the War on Marijuana

Count me as a fan of both Republican Ron Paul and Democrat Barney Frank, and not just because of their latest bill which would end the federal ban on marijuana and limit the federal government to patrolling for inter-state smuggling.

“The legislation would limit the federal government’s role in marijuana enforcement to cross-border or inter-state smuggling, allowing people to legally grow, use or sell marijuana in states where it is legal,” according to the Marijuana Policy Project, which advocates for pot legalization. “The legislation is the first bill ever introduced in Congress to end federal marijuana prohibition.”

Returning the legalization question to the states would have numerous benefits. It would allow states to handle the problems associated with drug use on their own, and allow legal medical marijuana dispensaries to operate without the threat of federal raids.

States could tax and regulate marijuana the same way they tax and regulate alcohol (we’ll leave aside the many stupid regulations surrounding alcohol sales for the time being). And patients prescribed medical marijuana would benefit as well.

With the federal ban lifted, states could fully legalize the sale of marijuana, legalize medical marijuana only, or experiment with other approaches. States that already legalize medical marijuana could continue without expensive legal battles with the federal government.

Furthermore, struggling state budgets could benefit from a decrease in spending on prisons and police and an increase in tax revenues from the sale of marijuana. Meanwhile, the federal government would save money as the scope of its operations were scaled back.

But most importantly, this would lead to a decrease in drug-related violence.

The Obama administration has promised it would scale back its efforts at overriding local marijuana laws, but this has not happened:

In March, for example, DEA agents raided two medical marijuana dispensaries in West Hollywood, California, and 26 dispensaries in 13 cities across Montana.

It’s time to make that promise official. Federalism would be far better than the federal overreach we have now.

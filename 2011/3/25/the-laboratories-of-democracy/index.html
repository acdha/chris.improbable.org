the laboratories of democracy

by russell

Folks with a federalist bent often refer to the various states as the "laboratories of democracy".  Let policy initiatives begin at a local or state level, where the issues are often more tangible to folks, and where the costs and risks are lower.  As thing get sorted out there, the best of breed ideas will bubble up to the national level.

It's not a bad idea.

Here in Massachusetts we've been conducting a laboratory exercise in health insurance provision for about five years.  Begun under Mitt Romney, who now can't run away from it fast enough, the high points of the MA plan are as follows:

  • everyone has to have coverage
  • if you're under 150% of poverty level, the state will provide it
  • If you're under 300% of poverty level, the state will help you buy it
  • if you don't acquire coverage through one means or another, you are hit with a tax penalty

Five years in, how's it working out?  Today's Boston Globe finds as follows (heads up - link may require registration):

  • Lots folks are opting for high-deductible, low-cost plans, because they're cheaper
  • Due to the high deductible (typically $1000 per year) folks are not getting preventive care
  • This happens even when deductibles are waived for preventive care, because most people don't read the fine print on their insurance policies

Some of the results:

But the percentage of young children receiving vaccinations dropped as much as 8.5 percent in the first year their families were covered by the high-deductible plans, while vaccination rates for children in standard plans rose slightly. And use of cancer screenings was two to three percentage points lower among families with high deductibles.

The takeaway quote:

“These plans are reducing costs substantially, but it’s not clear that they can maintain quality, and that’s the real concern,’’ said Amelia Haviland, coauthor of the RAND Corporation study.

So - as is often claimed, when folks are required to pay for services out of their own pocket, they often go without.  In fact, even if they *think* they may have to pay for them, they often go without.

Cost savings - win!

But the savings come at the expense of their actual health.  Lower vaccination rates, fewer cancer screenings, etc.

Not so much of a win.

If "Obamacare" survives the various conservative assaults being made on it, we're likely to be looking at a national experience somewhat like MA's.  For good or ill.

If it does not, the MA experiment, with all of its shortcomings, is probably going to be among the more generous regimes available.

Belgium beats Iraq

Belgium sets a dubious record on Thursday when it overtakes post-war Iraq as the country that has gone longest without a government.

It’s a surreal achievement greeted with a mix of amusement and quiet despair in the streets of Brussels. (I speak of “Brussels” as the capital of Belgium here – Eurocrats based in the city pay only passing attention to things Belgian).

For those not following the Kafkaesque saga that is Belgian politics, ever since the government collapsed on April 26th, and after Flemish nationalists became the country’s biggest party in the ensuing June elections, politicians have been unable to patch over linguistic and cultural differences that separate Dutch speakers in Flanders from Francophones in Wallonia.

The recently-settled Iraq dispute lasted 249 days after the Shiite party of sitting prime minister Nouri Maliki was finally returned to power in December, despite having fewer seats than a secular slate headed by former prime minister Iyad Allawi, which had the support of may of Iraq’s minority Sunnis.

If sectarian differences can be overcome in Iraq, perhaps they will someday be in Belgium, too. But no one is predicting it will happen any time soon.

The biggest party in Flanders, Bart De Wever’sNew Flemish Alliance, or N-VA, ran on a platform of securing more powers for its (already powerful) regional authority at the expense of the federal government.

The Walloons worry that fewer powers at federal level means less redistribution of tax money towards their southern region. Flanders – home to booming cities like Antwerp and Ghent – has become markedly richer in recent decades, an inversion of the time when Wallonia’s heavy industries made it the wealthier of the two.

De Wever and his allies – which now include most Flemish parties – insist on agreeing overarching state reform before a government can be formed, which has led to the 249-day delay.

What has happened in the last eight months, a time in which most countries can form a government and watch it fall out of public favour? A procession of political grandees have been appointed to advise King Albert II on how to exit this particular impasse, so far with little success.

Right now it’s the turn of newly-appointed Didier Reynders, a Wallonian liberal and the outgoing finance minister – like all cabinet ministers, he has been “outgoing” since the government collapsed in April – to try and broker some kind of agreement.

Reynders has his work cut out for him. The seven parties named early on as possible coalition partners have not met as a group since September. Much of the time has been spent in negotiations over how to negotiate. So even if there is a breakthrough, it could still be months before a government is formed.

Alternatively, the whole thing could be wrapped up in a few days.

But in political circles, there’s a growing belief that no compromise can be reached and another round of elections is likely. More precisely, they believe elections will be raised as a way to see the “final best offer” each political group has been holding back.

The N-VA, which was founded only ten years ago, has seen its poll numbers rise as it continues to hold out, however, so it has little incentive to seal a deal. The Francophone parties don’t want to give in too much from their normal orthodox line in case they have to stand for elections in the near future.

For the average Belgian, the absence of a government has barely been noticeable, however. Belgium ran the EU presidency in the second half of 2010 with aplomb. Its caretaker government has been able to tackle big crises (like when bond markets started wondering about its high debt levels) competently. Regional authorities make sure the trains run on time.

Some speculate that may in fact be Mr De Wever’s ploy: show that Belgium doesn’t need a federal government. Then the break-up of Belgium that his party is pushing for won’t seem like such a big deal.

If only Iraq were so easy.

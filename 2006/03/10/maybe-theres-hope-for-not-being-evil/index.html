Maybe there's hope for not being evil

Fortune's recent article about digital music marketshare contains some fairly well-known observations about the iTunes Music Store but also drops this zinger (emphasis mine):

The situation has been a disaster for Apple's competitors. iTunes holds a commanding lead over its rivals, selling more than 75% of all digital songs, according to NPD. The second-place digital music store, eMusic, can't sell any major label hits because it refuses to copy protect them. Instead, it relies on independent labels for content. But eMusic has a 9% share of the market, largely because you can play its unprotected MP3s on an iPod.

Microsoft, the recording oligarchy and the also-ran stores have made an enormous effort developing and tightly integrating what is arguably the best-in-breed DRM platform - and those restrictions have completely cancelled out the advantage of having popular, heavily-promoted music. I've been an eMusic subscriber since 2002 and have found a great deal of music which I've enjoyed but I still find it amazing to learn that they're beating out everyone other than iTunes using only indie labels.

There's an important lesson about the value of DRM: eMusic has succeeded because they've made a service which is more convenient and isn't predicated on the implication that you, the paying customer, are an inveterate thief who must be carefully restricted. Apple seems somewhat contradictory but they've enjoyed the combination of having the least intrusive DRM implementation, a wildly popular player and self-destructive competitors like Sony, leaving us with a general rule: at best DRM will not kill your product.

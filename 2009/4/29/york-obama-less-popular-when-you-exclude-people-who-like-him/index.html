York: Obama Less Popular When You Exclude People Who Like Him

by publius

Dave Weigel brings the snark to Bryon York's observation that Obama would be less popular if we pretend black people aren't part of America.  Here's the part that I find rather baffling:

On his 100th day in office, Barack Obama enjoys high job approval ratings, no matter what poll you consult. But if a new survey by the New York Times is accurate, the president and some of his policies are significantly less popular with white Americans than with black Americans, and his sky-high ratings among African-Americans make some of his positions appear a bit more popular overall than they actually are.


It's the last part that doesn't make sense.  If black Americans strongly support Obama's positions, how on earth does that make the "overall" support deceptively high?  I mean, black people are part of America "overall," right?  George Bush enjoyed very strong support among Southern white evangelicals.  Did that mean Bush's "overall" support was deceptively high?

I don't like throwing inflammatory words around on issues of race.  But there's got to be some label for this subconscious discounting of black votes -- and the implicit overvaluing of "real" (i.e., white) America.  It's just weird.

Ancestral virus gave wasps their parasitic power

Over 200,000 species of wasps reproduce in a parasitic manner by injecting their eggs into larvae along with a dose of virus-like particles that compromise the larvae’s immune system and overall health to help the eggs flourish. These virus-like particles, called polydnaviruses, behave only somewhat like real viruses. They have proteins that disrupt the host immune system, but they don’t encode for any other viral proteins. Their DNA is fully part of the wasp genome; they form only in wasp ovaries; and they don’t replicate inside host larvae. These unusual characteristics led scientists to question how these particles developed evolutionarily.

One side argues that polydnaviruses arose from wasp genes, while the other side hypothesizes that wasps benefited from an outside virus and ended up incorporating that virus into its own genome. In today’s issue of Science, Jean-Michel Drezen and 16 other researchers appeared to have settled the debate for at least one family of wasps, the braconids.

Troubling trends in the fish stalls - OregonLive.com

TOKYO -- Just before dawn a worker at Tsukiji, the world's largest fish market, steps up on a wooden stool and rings a cowbell. The clanging bell signals the opening of the daily auction of tuna, the most sought-after fish on earth.

These days it rings like an alarm. Tuna populations have collapsed under the overcapacity of fishing fleets, pirate fishing, the use of spotter planes to chase fish and the insatiable demand for luxury seafood. And though it's tuna that draws hundreds of bleary, jet-lagged Western tourists to Tsukiji's early morning auctions, the frozen hulks of bluefin laid out on the concrete floor tell only part of the story of a global fisheries crisis that reaches from the Oregon coast to Tokyo Harbor.

The rest is found in the 1,700 stalls of this massive, chaotic market that sells more than 500 species of fish and other sea creatures annually worth more than $5.5 billion. At Tsukiji, pronounced "skee-jee," you see the shrinking size of fish and hear the warnings of worried wholesalers, retailers and sushi chefs. Most are following in the footsteps of their fathers and grandfathers, and nearly all now fear that the fish -- and their businesses -- won't survive to be passed on to their children.

"The Japanese have always had the belief that fish are like the water, like the air, available all the time," said Masayuki Komatsu, a former leader in Japan's Ministry of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries and now a professor at Tokyo's National Graduate Institute for Policy Studies. "We thought they would last forever."

Why Reform Is Hard

by publius

I'm glad Ezra Klein wrote this, because it helps provide context for increasingly disillusioned liberals who think Obama and the Dem leadership are blowing health care.  The upshot is that it's hard to say things like "if the bill isn't sufficiently progressive, we'll oppose it" when that's exactly what the other side wants.  Here's Ezra:

The problem, I think, is that there is a tendency to understand heath-care reform as an equal negotiation in which all sides want a deal, and you can game out various bargaining stratagems. But health-care reform is not a negotiation. It's a campaign.

"Walking away" is a great strategy only when the other side needs you at the table.  Think about the iPhone deal between Apple and AT&T.  If AT&T asks for too much, then Apple will walk.  But because AT&T really wants the iPhone on its network, it will modify its position.  Same deal in reverse.  If Apple asks for too much, then AT&T will walk.  But because Apple wants a deal (and subsidies, etc.), it will eventually modify its position.

That's not at all how health coverage reform negotiations are structured.  If the progressives threaten to walk, Republicans and Blue Dogs will say, "fantastic, don't let the door hit you."  Accordingly, the prospect of not passing reform provides virtually no leverage with these people -- particularly as the bill gets picked apart day after day by slanders, complacent media, and delays.  Thanks Max!

Another point that Ezra raises is that progressives really really want reform to pass.  Virtually anything that can get 60 Dems + Snowe/Collins would exponentially improve the status quo.  And so progressives would be foolish to walk away.

I don't really know what this analysis implies in terms of actual tactics and strategy.  It's just one of the many reasons why health care reform is really hard to do, and really needs to be fought for -- whether you're the President, or whether you're correcting lies on Facebook or talking to friends at the Little League game.

Political idealism isn't about self-validation and catharsis.  It's about elbow grease, and volunteering, and sweat.  It's the difference between JFK idealism and Ted Kennedy idealism.  Ted's accomplishes things.  JFK's makes you feel good about yourself.  Obama's election made us all feel good about ourselves.  But if we're going to get something done, we'll need more Ted.  More fight.  It's hard because it's important.

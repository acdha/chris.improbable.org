Heavy budget cuts imminent for UK science

In the past week, budget numbers have come out of Congress for NIH and NSF that suggest both science agencies will do OK in the coming year; across the pond things aren’t looking nearly as rosy. The UK is in the midst of a severe budget crisis, with looming public sector cuts and tax increases aplenty. Higher education research budgets are set to be cut by almost a billion dollars between now and 2013. Referring to the UK government, the BBC reports, "It said £600m would come 'from higher education and science and research budgets from a combination of changes to student support within existing arrangements; efficiency savings and prioritisation across universities, science and research; some switching of modes of study in higher education; and reductions in budgets that do not support student participation.'"

The UK government claims that, despite the cuts, it remains committed to science, having doubled annual spending to ~$10 billion/year compared to the previous Tory government. Labour’s science credentials don’t look great though, after the controversy that erupted from the controversial "Nutt Sacking." Professor David Nutt, a well respected researcher who chaired the Advisory Council on the Misuse of Drugs. Nutt had the temerity to point out that, using evidence and science as a guide, tobacco and alcohol were more damaging to individuals and society than cannabis or ecstasy. Since that message wasn’t deemed politically acceptable, Nutt got the sack, and several other members of the committee resigned in protest.

The announced cuts are not final; they came as part of a pre-budget report, and would presumably happen should Labour win another term in the upcoming UK general election. With pundits suggesting that a hung parliament is the best the party can hope for, that’s by no means certain.

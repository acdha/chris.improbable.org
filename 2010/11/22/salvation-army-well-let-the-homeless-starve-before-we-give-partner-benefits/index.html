Salvation Army: We'll Let The Homeless Starve Before We Give Partner Benefits

Good riddance

The Salvation Army is threatening to close its New York City soup kitchens if the city enacts pending legislation requiring companies that do business with the city to offer domestic partner benefits. That's right, God's Gentle Loving Peopleâ„¢ would rather see the homeless dead on the streets, than stop discriminating.
The evangelical faith is prepared to give up the $70 million a year in city funding it receives and pull out of New York entirely the New York Post reports. With soup kitchens, shelters, foster care programs, and HIV services the Salvation Army is one of the largest private aid groups in New York serving some 5 million people annually. The Salvation Army has several multiyear contracts with the city totaling $250 million. Six city contracts worth $12.8 million are to expire June 30, by which time the bill may be law. New York City council overwhelmingly approved the legislation earlier this month, and it is sitting on the desk of mayor Michael Bloomberg. Bloomberg is opposed to the bill, even though his own company offers partner benefits. Even if the mayor vetoes the legislation, the support in council is considered sufficient to override it.
RELATED: San Francisco has long had a similar ordinance to one proposed in New York City. In 1998, the Salvation Army turned down a multimillion dollar contract with San Francisco rather than comply. In 2001, the organization announced that it would begin offering domestic partner benefits in their western region, which covers 13 states, but they rescinded that offer a mere two weeks later after a firestorm of criticism from other anti-gay groups.

Our Hard Working Senators

-by Sebastian

The Three-Day Work Week

Matthew Yglesias focuses on one of the real reasons why the Senate can't possibly be bothered to spend a fews days or a week to even attempt to break a real filibuster--if they even bothered to make the minority party mount one:

All this activity is crammed into a three-day week, for it’s an unwritten rule of the modern Senate that votes are almost never scheduled for Mondays or Fridays, which allows senators to spend four days away from the capital. Senators now, unlike those of several decades ago, often keep their families in their home states, where they return most weekends, even if it’s to Alaska or Idaho—a concession to endless fund-raising, and to the populist anti-Washington mood of recent years. (When Newt Gingrich became Speaker of the House, in 1995, he told new Republican members not to move their families to the capital.) Tom Daschle, the former Democratic leader, said, “When we scheduled votes, the only day where we could be absolutely certain we had all one hundred senators there was Wednesday afternoon.”
This combines with 10-14 weeks a year in recess, to make it impossible to have a long period of deliberating/considering/mounting a serious effort on anything.  

I know it must be horribly taxing to have to personally attend fund raisers, which are absolutely mandatory when incumbency carries such a ridiculously high built-in advantage (generally over 90% reelection rates); but maybe, if you really want to get something done, you could pick an important topic, and make  some small attempt to break the routine threat of filibusters instead of having a 3 day work week every single week during the merely 3/4 of the year that you aren't in weeks-long recess.  

And if after a month or two of actually working 5 whole days in a row you still couldn't do it, then I might entertain the idea that the part of the system which is broken isn't the part that lets you be too lazy to make an effort to do your job.

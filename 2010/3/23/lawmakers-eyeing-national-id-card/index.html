Lawmakers Eyeing National ID Card

This isn't a popular position but I say bring it on: we have have a number of mildly-to-incredibly half-assed ID card systems, none of which have prevented the surveillance state from expanding rapidly. Since we invented computers, the benefit to be had from multiple agencies issuing IDs is about zero, rising higher only if gross incompetence counts as a privacy measure.

What we need is a corresponding set of privacy laws and actual accountability for misuse which rely on the concept of demonstrating successful handling of data rather than treating the possibility of error or malice as unlikely occurrences to be dealt with on a one-off basis. This data will be collected and searched - it's time we actually accepted that and thought about how to maintain an acceptable level of privacy.

Lawmakers are proposing a national identification card — what they’re calling “high-tech, fraud-proof Social Security cards” required for all employees in the United States.

The proposal by Sen. Charles Schumer (D-New York) and Sen. Lindsay Graham (R-South Carolina) comes as the states are grappling with producing another national identification card at the behest of the Department of Homeland Security. Virtually none of the states are in compliance with this Real ID program — adopted in 2005 — requiring state motor vehicle bureaus to obtain and internally scan and store personal information like Social Security cards and birth certificates for a national database.

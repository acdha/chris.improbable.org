African corruption saps 25% of GDP

The African Union released a study about corruption in Africa. The BBC reports:

Corruption is illegal everywhere in Africa, but everywhere it is woven deep into the fabric of every day life.

From the bottle of whisky slipped under the counter to speed a traveller's way through customs, to the presidents and ex-presidents living way beyond their declared means, it results in an assumption that no business will ever get done without a present changing hands.

The report before this week's meeting of the African Union in the Ethiopian capital, Addis Ababa, makes no attempt to excuse these "gratifications" as part of the culture.

It says that corruption is costing Africa more than $148bn dollars a year, increasing the cost of goods by as much as 20%, deterring investment and holding back development.

Most of the cost, it says, falls on the poor.

The Economist notes that this "is about 25% of Africa's GDP".

This problem is why why proposals like NEPAD (BBC, ABCNews) are unlikely to suceed - handing over billions (which Nigerian President Olusegun Obasanjo disingenuously refers to as Africa "lift[ing] itself by its own bootstrap") with only promises that it will be handled responsibly is just going to enrich the people who created the problems in the first place. Outside accountability is needed but for some odd reason the governments who'd have act responsibly aren't in favor of it...

That BBC article illustrates why NEPAD's stated goal of self-policing is likely to be a sham:

Western governments, together with a clutch of non-governmental organisations including the Commonwealth, cast doubt on the legitimacy of the President Robert Mugabe's victory.

Zimbabwe's neighbours, however, were broadly in agreement that Mr Mugabe won fairly.

Jacob Zuma, the South African deputy president even lectured the west about "collective punishment":

There is a tendency to look at one country and say that it is every country in Africa... to almost want to punish all countries in Africa collectively ... Zimbabwe was such an example. If one country has done certain things... you must not try to also punish other people.

Collective bad consequences for collectively supporting a corrupt thug's rigged elections - how terrible! It's not like there's much room for legitimate support here - we're talking about someone who deliberately starves regions which didn't vote for him and is busy destroying commercial in a country on the edge of a food crisis simply to reward his cronies with farmland which is now left idle.

The West has failed badly in giving financial aid without strict links to progress on civil and economic freedom. As the African Union study shows deciding to ignore governmental corruption in an attempt to help the poor anyway merely delays real improvments.

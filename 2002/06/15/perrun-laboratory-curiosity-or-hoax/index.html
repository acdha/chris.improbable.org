Perrun: laboratory curiosity or hoax?

Computer viruses are notoriously overhyped by the media. An excellent example is a recent story about a "JPEG virus" ( DigitaMASS, ComputerWire (via The Register), Associated Press (Wired), CNET) which raises the specter of virus infected data files:

Once you get past the hype, it turns out that this isn't anything new. Perrun depends on a normal virus being installed on a computer to read those JPEG files! Once a virus successfully infects a computer, it can by definition do anything it wants, including installing code hidden inside data files. This thing spreads like any other virus and can easily be stopped using the same anti-virus technology we've been using for many years.

Anti-virus companies have a long history of grossly exagerating virus threats in an attempt to scare money out of users. One of the most common tactics is pointing out threats which are very unlikely but theoretically possible, as demonstrated by an NAI spokesperson:

In its current form, an infected JPG file cannot infect another computer on its own. But Gullotto said there's no reason a virus writer couldn't make the picture itself able to infect other computers.

There is a major reason why this isn't much of a threat: JPEG files aren't executable. That's why Perrun needs a standalone program to run those trojaned images. It's theoretically possible that this could change - Microsoft added executable content to Office documents and email with disastrous results - but it would require a significant change in the nature of the JPEG format and wide industry support. There's no indication that any of this is actually happening - just a scary warning from a company which coincidentally is selling a product billed as the solution.

IBM's new 64-bit desktop PowerPC

IBM is presenting a new PowerPC aimed desktops and low-end servers. This has kicked off a huge frenzy in Mac land and I think there some are interesting possibilities for those of us in the scientific computing world. IBM has made a major Linux investment and is selling quite a few clusters. Their top chip is the Power4 but it's a pricey beast which costs more than one of our 1U dual-processor nodes. Athlons offer exceptional price performance but produce a lot of heat. If IBM's ships a chip in a reasonable timeframe which offers anything like decent FPU/vector performance and continues the PowerPC tradition of running cooler than the x86 chips, IBM could have an interesting option in the cluster market.

One nice trend we're seeing is much higher bandwidth - 6.4GB/s for Hypertransport coupled with major efficiency improvements. Until the P4 price/performance drops or SSE2 optimization becomes automatic, Intel's best cluster chip is the venerable but bandwidth-challenged PIII. AMD's doing a bit better with the current Athlons but the real action will come with the Hammer chips using Hypertransport and on-chip memory controllers (4 CPUs get peak DDR bandwidth each, not total as with most current systems).

Memory bandwidth and 64-bit computing are still largely non-issues for typical desktop users but have become critical for scientists who frequently need to process extremely large data sets or complex simulations. It's frustrating to realize that all of your nice, high-performance CPUs are sitting idle because data can't arrive as quickly as they process it. Both AMD's Hammer and IBM's new PowerPC offer the possibility of affordable high-bandwidth which could easily double or triple real-world performance.

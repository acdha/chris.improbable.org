Different perspectives

If you live in the UK, your legal systemis about to get a lot less friendly with fewer jury trials, no double-jeopardy protections and increased police powers.

It's interesting to note that while everyone is bitching about the US backing out of the ICC or lost civil rights post 9-11, both of which are still largely hypothetical concerns at this point, there seem to be only a few groups warning about things which are actually happening in the rest of the first world.

This is particularly interesting in the context of the ICC - ignoring all of the philosophical and political concerns, the US government is constitutionally unable to support it. Signed treaties become law here and, as has been discussed elsewhere, the ICC rules would violate a number of key constitutional protections including trial by jury and protection from self-incrimination. What amazes is me is that everyone's acting like staying out of a major civil rights setback is a bad thing.

While tattered in places, the U.S. Bill of Rights still seems to be leading most of the world, particularly given that its rights cannot be changed on a whim by anyone in power. The biggest difference is in the view of where rights come from - in the US, they're traditionally considered the "unalienable rights" of any human whereas in most of the world they're still more commonly seen as something granted to citizens by the government. Watching accepted protections disappear as the Eurocrats test their power demonstrates why those reassurances which boil down to "Nobody would ever misuse these powers" aren't very satisfying.

This is extremely disappointing as the US is far from perfect. The currently accepted protections should be seen as a bare minimum, a foundation for further improvement. The war on drugs and the federal government's power grab from the states have led to quite a few ongoing battles. We should be trying to continue moving things forward; unfortunately, most of the world seems determined to go back and rediscover the reasons for restrictions on government power the hard way.
